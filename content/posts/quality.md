Quality is often treated as a role rather than a responsibility.

In many engineering organisations, quality sits with QA. Tests are written elsewhere. Bugs are found late. Engineers “hand over” work once it is functionally complete and move on. When something escapes, quality becomes a discussion about process rather than ownership.

I have worked in organisations where this model functioned well, and in others where it quietly undermined accountability. The difference was not the presence of QA, but how responsibility for quality was framed.

Quality cannot be delegated away from the people who build the system.

At its best, QA is a force multiplier. At its worst, it becomes a buffer that allows engineers to disengage from the consequences of what they ship. When quality is seen as someone else’s job, it creates a throw-it-over-the-wall mentality that scales surprisingly quickly.

Engineers optimise for passing work on. QA optimises for catching issues. The system optimises for friction.

The most effective organisations I have seen treat quality as an engineering responsibility first and a specialised function second. Engineers own the correctness, reliability, and behaviour of the systems they build. QA exists to strengthen that ownership, not replace it.

This distinction matters.

When engineers are accountable for quality, behaviour changes upstream. Designs become simpler. Edge cases are considered earlier. Tests are written closer to intent rather than bolted on later. Bugs feel personal, not abstract.

It also changes how teams respond when things go wrong. There is less defensiveness, because the line of responsibility is clear. Quality issues are investigated as system failures, not as misses by another team.

That does not mean there is no place for QA.

Dedicated QA roles bring deep expertise in test strategy, exploratory testing, automation frameworks, and user-centric validation that many engineers do not naturally develop. They are often better at seeing the product the way a customer does, rather than the way the system expects to be used.

In complex systems, that perspective is invaluable.

The mistake is using QA as a safety net rather than as a partner.

When QA is positioned as the final gate, they are forced into an adversarial role. They become the people who slow releases down, say no, and raise bugs when deadlines are already tight. This creates tension, not quality.

In healthier organisations, QA works earlier and more collaboratively. They help define quality bars. They influence testability during design. They identify risk areas before code is written. They focus on what is hard to automate and easy to miss.

Crucially, engineers still ship with their name on the outcome.

As organisations scale, this model becomes even more important. Centralised QA does not scale linearly with engineering output. What does scale is ownership embedded within teams. Engineers who understand that they are responsible not just for features, but for the long-term health of what they build.

This is also where leadership matters. If leaders talk about quality but reward speed alone, teams will respond accordingly. If incidents and defects are treated as execution failures rather than learning opportunities, engineers will push risk downstream.

Quality culture is shaped by what leaders tolerate.

My preference is simple. Engineers should own quality by default. QA should exist to raise the bar, not carry it. When both are aligned, quality becomes something the organisation builds into the system, not something it inspects in at the end.

The goal is not fewer bugs. The goal is fewer surprises.

When engineers are accountable for what they release, quality stops being a phase and starts being a habit.
