Leverage is what allows an engineering organisation to grow without collapsing under its own weight.

At senior levels, your job is no longer to do more work. It is to make the organisation capable of doing more work without you. That only happens when you deliberately design for leverage.

Many organisations talk about scaling. Fewer understand leverage. Scaling often means adding people, process, or layers. Leverage means increasing output per unit of effort. The two are not the same, and confusing them leads to bloated organisations that move slowly despite having talented people.

Leverage starts with clarity of outcomes.

Teams do not need more direction. They need clearer constraints and better context. When engineers understand what problem actually matters, they make better local decisions without escalation. When they understand what does not matter, they stop over engineering.

This is why senior leaders who obsess over vision and priorities create disproportionate impact. A well articulated direction removes hundreds of micro decisions across the organisation. That is leverage.

The next source of leverage is ownership.

High leverage organisations push decision making as close to the work as possible. Not because leaders do not care, but because centralised decision making does not scale. Clear ownership enables teams to move independently while still pulling in the same direction.

This requires trust, and trust is built by holding people accountable for outcomes, not activity. When ownership is real, escalation drops. Decisions happen faster. Leaders stop being bottlenecks.

Systems and platforms are another powerful form of leverage.

Every repeated manual task is a signal. Either the work is not important enough to automate, or it is important enough that it should not rely on human memory. High performing organisations invest aggressively in internal tooling, platforms, and abstractions that reduce cognitive load and remove accidental complexity.

The return on this investment compounds. A small platform team can unlock productivity across dozens of product teams. This is not about central control. It is about enabling speed at the edges.

Talent density matters more than headcount.

A small number of high leverage individuals can outperform much larger teams when placed correctly. Staff and Principal engineers, strong engineering managers, and technical leaders who multiply others are worth orders of magnitude more than their individual output.

Leverage here comes from amplification. Mentorship. Clear standards. Technical direction. Raising the bar quietly but consistently. The mistake is treating senior talent as just more hands rather than as force multipliers.

Process can create leverage, but only when it removes friction.

Most organisations add process to feel safe. High leverage organisations add process to make the right thing easier. Good process codifies lessons learned and makes failure modes harder to repeat. Bad process exists to compensate for missing trust or clarity.

If a process does not reduce cognitive load or decision cost, it is probably negative leverage.

Another often overlooked source of leverage is decision hygiene.

Senior leaders make fewer decisions than they think. What they really do is decide how decisions get made. Who decides. With what information. On what timeline. This meta decision making determines whether the organisation moves smoothly or thrashes.

Clear decision frameworks and escalation paths prevent ambiguity from becoming drag. People know when to act, when to ask, and when to stop.

Finally, leverage comes from narrative.

What leaders repeat becomes culture. What they ignore becomes acceptable. A consistent narrative around quality, ownership, reliability, and outcomes shapes behaviour even when no one is watching. This is slow leverage, but it is incredibly powerful.

Over time, the organisation starts to self correct. New hires adapt faster. Teams align without meetings. Standards propagate without enforcement.

This is what sustainable leverage looks like.

At Director level and above, leverage is the difference between being busy and being effective. It is what allows you to operate at scale without burning out yourself or the organisation.

If you find yourself in every decision, every incident, every review, that is not commitment. It is a signal that leverage is missing.

Build clarity. Push ownership. Invest in systems. Back multipliers. Remove friction. Shape the narrative.

Do this well, and the organisation will grow in capability faster than it grows in size.

That is the goal.
